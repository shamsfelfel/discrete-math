The Foundations: Logic and Proofs

The rules of logic give precise meaning to mathematical statements and underpin every mathematical argument. This chapter develops propositional and predicate logic, then introduces the methods of proof that the rest of the book — and most of mathematics — depends on.

Lessons

Pick a lesson to begin. Each combines flashcard-style concept cards with graded practice questions.
1.1

Propositional Logic

Propositions, the logical connectives (negation, conjunction, disjunction, conditional, biconditional), truth tables, and operator precedence.
1.2

Applications of Propositional Logic

Translating English into logic, system specifications, Boolean searches, logic puzzles, and the basics of logic circuits.
1.3

Propositional Equivalences

Tautologies, contradictions, logical equivalence, the standard equivalence laws, De Morgan's laws, and proving equivalence by transformation.
1.4

Predicates and Quantifiers

Predicates, the universal (∀) and existential (∃) quantifiers, the domain of discourse, and negating quantified statements with De Morgan's laws.
1.5

Nested Quantifiers

Statements with multiple quantifiers, why the order matters (∀x∃y vs ∃y∀x), translating English, and negating nested quantified statements.
1.6

Rules of Inference

Valid arguments, the standard rules (modus ponens, modus tollens, hypothetical and disjunctive syllogism, resolution), and rules of inference for quantified statements.
1.7

Introduction to Proofs

Theorems, axioms, and definitions; direct proofs, proofs by contraposition, vacuous and trivial proofs, and proofs by contradiction.
1.8

Proof Methods and Strategy

Proof by cases, exhaustive proof, "without loss of generality," constructive vs. nonconstructive existence, uniqueness proofs, counterexamples, and finding proofs.
