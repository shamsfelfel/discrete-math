Chapter 9

Relations

A relation is a set of ordered pairs — the most general way to link elements of sets. This chapter studies binary relations and their properties, n-ary relations, how to represent relations with matrices and digraphs, closures, and equivalence relations.

Lessons

Choose a section to study.
Lesson 9.1
Relations and Their Properties
Binary relations as subsets of a Cartesian product; reflexive, symmetric, antisymmetric and transitive properties; combining relations, composites and powers.
Lesson 9.2
n-ary Relations and Their Applications
Relations linking elements of more than two sets — their degree and domains — and the relational database model they underpin: records, primary keys, selection, projection, and join.
Lesson 9.3
Representing Relations
Two compact representations of a relation on finite sets: the zero–one matrix and the directed graph (digraph), and how each reveals reflexivity, symmetry, antisymmetry, and transitivity.
Lesson 9.4
Closures of Relations
The smallest relation with a desired property that contains a given one: the reflexive closure (add the diagonal), the symmetric closure (add the inverse), and the transitive closure via paths and the connectivity relation.
Lesson 9.5
Equivalence Relations
Relations that are reflexive, symmetric, and transitive — equivalence relations — together with equivalence classes and the partition they induce on a set.
